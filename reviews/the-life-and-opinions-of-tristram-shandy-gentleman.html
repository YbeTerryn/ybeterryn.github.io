Laurence Stern's experimental anti-biography of Tristam Shandy should rightly be applauded for it's sheer innovation, providing the eighteenth century Englishmen with nine volumes of nonsensical storymaking.  
'The Life and Opinions of Tristram Shandy, Gentleman' is a very early toe dipping in post-modernism. A daring experiment, but an experiment still. 
One of the first novels to ever use a meta perspective, but certainly not the best example of it. It is very hard to follow at times (might be due to English not being my first language, although others novels did not pose much problems in that regard). This makes that the jokes don't always land. It is not an enjoyable read from start to finish. There are places where Tristam's mumbling actually is entertaining and reads properly, but there are more instances where one has to fight the urge to skip parts or put the convoluted mess completely aside. 
Laurence Sterns's idea was strong but the execution leaves one wondering.
