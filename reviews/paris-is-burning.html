What makes Paris Is Burning's message so poignant, is that it doesn't overtly chase the message. The documentary doesn't shy away from the streets, the rivalry, the fight's, the prostitution, the crime, but it also never exploits any of those things. In fact we get a fairly intimate portrait of different people and their search for identity. We see goals achieved and dreams turned into nightmares. From silent military stoicism to outlandish 'xtravanganza'. Participating in a ball, one can be one, neither or both. A free way of expressing's one dream, to be recognized as what you could be. From a somewhat vulgar and lewd butt shaking to genuinely skillful 'vogueing'. Fame in it's smallest, but not less meaningful, form It's about family, acceptance, conflicting ideas, gender and most of all life itself. While often used to prove the binding human experience is felt by everyone, including transgenders, gays and the whole spectrum, the film can also be used to argue that a ball is not far from middle class life. The very executive-looking, with an executive-approved haircut and an executive-fitting tie, corporate executive is just like the people who he would condescendingly refer to as 'freaks', putting on a show for the world to applaud, to confirm his identity. And maybe those so-called freaks are simply much more honest about it.
